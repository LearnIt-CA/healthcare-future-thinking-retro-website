So I got to try one of those new surgical simulators last month during a lab visit. It's basically a video game controller hooked up to a box that pushes back when you press on virtual tissue. You practice laparoscopic techniques on a screen before you ever touch a real patient.

Honest reaction: it felt like a video game. And I mean that in a good way. I'm a decent gamer. I picked up the motions fast. But my attending (who's been doing this for 20 years) struggled more than I did at first. That felt significant to me.

I know anecdotes aren't data. But is this the direction things are going? Are hospitals going to start caring whether you grew up with a Nintendo?

Surgery is not a video game. Never will be. The things a simulator cannot teach you: bleeding that doesn't stop on schedule, tissue that doesn't behave like the model, a patient who moved. The unpredictability is the whole point.

I don't doubt that simulators have some value for basic motor skill drills. But the idea that being good at Zelda makes you a better surgeon is the kind of thing that sounds smart in a coffee shop and falls apart in an OR. Clinical judgment comes from experience with real patients, not from racking up simulator scores.

— "A good surgeon knows how to operate; a great surgeon knows when not to."

People forget this technology didn't come out of nowhere. DARPA funded the original remote surgery research in the 1990s specifically for battlefield use: operating on wounded soldiers too dangerous to move, via robot, from a safe location. The Lindbergh Operation in 2001 was essentially a civilian demonstration of military technology that had been in development for a decade.

The question now isn't can it work; we know it can. The question is who builds the network infrastructure that makes it work at scale, outside of a one-off $1M private fiber cable. The technology leads. The economics lag. This is always how it goes.

The technology concern isn't the cable. It's the latency. The Lindbergh team got below 200 milliseconds over that private line. But surgeons need feedback in under 200ms to work safely; 300ms feels "wrong" and could cause errors. On commercial internet infrastructure, you regularly see 150–400ms depending on load. The variance is the problem, not the average.

You can't have a surgeon in the middle of a critical maneuver and then have a half-second delay spike because someone in their ISP's area is downloading a Linux distribution. This isn't a solvable problem by making the cable better. It's a network architecture problem.

— Hospitalist. "Perfect is the enemy of good. Except in surgery."

The comparison baseline matters. You're comparing da Vinci to "skilled conventional laparoscopy," which takes years to develop and has its own steep learning curve. Compare da Vinci to open surgery, which is the realistic alternative for most procedures, and the picture changes: smaller incisions, less blood loss, shorter hospital stays. Those translate directly to patient outcomes and cost savings that offset the capital expenditure over 5–10 years.

Also: the RCT ship has sailed. You're not going to get informed consent for a trial where half the patients get a worse surgical approach. The evidence base will be observational data from here.
