GLOBAL HEALTH
World Health Organization Declares Obesity a Global Epidemic
🖶 Print  |  ✉ Email  |  🔗 Share

GENEVA, Switzerland — The World Health Organization has published a major report officially declaring obesity a global epidemic, warning that rates are climbing fast in both rich and poor countries alike. The report follows a 1997 WHO consultation that first raised the alarm, and officials say the trend shows no sign of slowing in any region of the world.

Three years after the diet drug combination fen-phen was pulled from shelves across North America over heart valve damage, the report notes that public confidence in pharmaceutical solutions for weight loss has fallen sharply just as the underlying problem keeps growing.

"We have a rapidly worsening health crisis and almost no medical tool that people trust to fix it," said one WHO nutrition advisor involved in drafting the report.

The report stops short of calling obesity a disease in its own right, a position some researchers argue is already out of date.
The Daily Health Tribune MARCH 2003 Toronto Bureau

A Joint Early-Warning Network Across Asia Helps World Track New Illness Reaching Toronto
🖶 Print  |  ✉ Email  |  🔗 Share

TORONTO — A severe respiratory illness has reached Toronto, where suspected cases are climbing day by day. Health authorities across Asia, including teams in China, Vietnam, and Singapore, have been sharing case data and lab findings through a joint regional early-warning network, now being credited with giving international health officials a head start on tracking how the illness, now being called SARS, spreads. Several Toronto hospitals have begun isolating patients and screening visitors as a precaution.

Health officials say the speed of the spread is still testing readiness everywhere it reaches. Canada has no single national agency dedicated to coordinating a response like this one, with responsibility currently split unevenly between the federal government and the provinces, a gap public health advocates have been pointing to for years.

The World Health Organization is monitoring the outbreak closely and has praised the coordinated regional response across Asia, calling the rapid information-sharing between national health authorities a model for how the rest of the world should respond to a fast-moving outbreak.
The Globe and Mail 2001 Foreign Desk, Geneva
MENTAL HEALTH
World Health Report Puts Mental Illness on the Global Agenda for the First Time
🖶 Print  |  ✉ Email  |  🔗 Share

GENEVA, Switzerland — For the first time in its history, the World Health Organization has dedicated its entire annual World Health Report to mental health, declaring it a leading and growing cause of disability worldwide. The report estimates that hundreds of millions of people live with a mental health condition, with the burden falling hardest on countries that can least afford to treat it.

The report notes that many countries in Africa and Asia currently spend less than one percent of their total health budgets on mental health care, despite carrying a heavy share of the global burden. Wealthier countries fare only somewhat better, with most public and employer health plans still treating mental health coverage as a lesser benefit than coverage for physical illness.

In Canada, there is still no national mental health agency or strategy as of this year, leaving each province to set its own funding and approach.
