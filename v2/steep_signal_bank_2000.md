# Healthcare Signal Bank, 2000
## What the World Looked Like, Cluster by Cluster

Three clusters. Each one shows where things stood in the year 2000, plus a short prediction of where it's heading. The signals underneath are real facts and real professional opinions from around that time, each one looking at the cluster from a different angle (a global report, a funding fight, a cultural shift, an expert warning, a system problem), so together they build a fuller picture than any single fact could on its own.

A matching document, `steep_outcomes_2025.md`, shows what actually happened in each of these three areas by 2025.

## 1. Obesity & Weight-Loss Care

**Where this is heading:** In 2000, doctors have nothing to offer people with obesity except advice to eat less and move more. In the next twenty years, new drugs will turn obesity into a treatable medical condition.

**Signals:**

- Since the World Health Organization's 1997 consultation, global obesity rates have been climbing steadily in both rich and poor countries, prompting the agency to formally label it an epidemic in its 2000 report.
- Confidence in pharmaceutical fixes for weight loss has been collapsing since the diet drug combination fen-phen was pulled from markets worldwide in 1997 over heart valve damage, even as public demand for some real medical answer keeps growing.
- The low-carbohydrate Atkins diet's popularity has exploded over just a few years, with sales climbing fast enough that bookstores can't keep it in stock.
- A small but growing number of researchers have begun arguing obesity should be treated as a chronic disease, pushing back against the long-standing mainstream view that it's a lifestyle failure.
- Demand for bariatric surgery in Canada is rising faster than hospitals can keep up with, and wait times are stretching longer every year.
- New government figures show childhood obesity in the UK has roughly doubled over the past decade, with officials blaming a generation raised on more processed food and less outdoor play.
- Japan's obesity rate remains under 5 percent this year, far below most of North America, with researchers pointing to traditional diet and portion sizes as part of the gap, though some worry that is starting to change.

## 2. Pandemic, Public Health & Vaccines

**Where this is heading:** In 2000, Canada has no national agency built to handle a serious disease outbreak. Within a few decades, a global pandemic will prove that a long-ignored vaccine idea actually works.

**Signals:**

- SARS cases in Toronto climb day by day through early 2003, eventually reaching 438 suspected cases and 44 deaths before the outbreak is contained.
- Pressure has been building for years for Canada to create a single national public health agency, as outbreak-response responsibility stays split unevenly between Ottawa and the provinces.
- A Hungarian-American scientist named Katalin Karikó has had her funding applications for mRNA research rejected year after year, even as she keeps refining the idea rather than abandoning it.
- Public health officials warn that emergency-readiness funding tends to spike right after a crisis grabs headlines, then fade until the next one hits.
- Childhood vaccination rates have been climbing for decades across Canada and most wealthy countries, with public trust in health agencies' vaccine guidance near an all-time high.
- The World Health Organization has launched a new Global Outbreak Alert and Response Network this year, linking laboratories and health agencies worldwide so a fast-moving illness can be tracked the moment it appears anywhere.
- The United Nations Security Council has formally declared HIV and AIDS a threat to international peace and security, the first time the council has ever treated a health crisis this way, reflecting the epidemic's toll across parts of Africa.

## 3. Mental Health

**Where this is heading:** In 2000, Canada has no national mental health strategy and most people stay quiet about therapy or medication. In the next two decades, mental health will move from a private shame to a public conversation that governments and workplaces can no longer ignore.

**Signals:**

- The World Health Organization dedicates its entire 2001 World Health Report to mental health for the first time, declaring it a leading and growing cause of disability worldwide.
- Canada has no national mental health agency or strategy in 2000, leaving each province to set its own funding and approach on its own.
- Prescriptions for SSRIs like Prozac and Zoloft have been climbing steadily through the 1990s across Canada and most wealthy countries.
- Surveys of teenagers show most still wouldn't tell classmates if they were seeing a therapist or taking medication, fearing judgment.
- Mental health coverage under most provincial and employer health plans remains far thinner than coverage for physical illness, with strict limits on therapy visits.
- The Royal College of Psychiatrists in the UK has launched a five-year public awareness campaign called "Changing Minds," aiming to challenge stigma around mental illness, one of the first campaigns of its kind anywhere.
- Mental health related sick leave is rising sharply across workplaces in Europe and North America, with employers increasingly citing stress and burnout, though most benefit plans still don't treat it as seriously as a physical injury.
