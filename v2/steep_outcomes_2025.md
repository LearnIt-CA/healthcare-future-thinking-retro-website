# Healthcare Outcomes, 2025
## What Actually Happened, Cluster by Cluster

This is not a signal bank. The 2000 document (`steep_signal_bank_2000.md`) is built from weak, ambiguous signals, things that could have gone in more than one direction. This document is the opposite: confirmed, real-world outcomes for each of the same three clusters, as of 2025. Use it to check predictions against reality, not to scan for clues.

---

## 1. Obesity & Weight-Loss Care

Worldwide adult obesity has more than doubled since 1990. By 2025, over 1 billion people globally live with obesity, and the epidemic the WHO warned about years ago didn't slow down, it accelerated.

A new class of drugs called GLP-1s, including Ozempic and Wegovy, reversed decades of public distrust in pharmaceutical fixes for weight loss. Roughly 10 million Americans alone are now taking one, and the global market for these drugs is worth more than $80 billion a year.

In 2020, Obesity Canada officially redefined obesity as a complex chronic disease in its national clinical guidelines, exactly the shift a small group of researchers had started pushing for. But the recognition stayed mostly symbolic: only Alberta's provincial government actually classifies obesity as a disease, so most Canadians still can't get GLP-1 drugs covered by public health plans.

Wait times for bariatric surgery in Canada remain long, and surgical programs are now also competing for resources against a flood of patients seeking GLP-1 prescriptions instead.

The Atkins-style low-carb diet craze faded within a decade, replaced first by other diet trends and eventually overtaken almost entirely by drug-based solutions. The bestselling diet book that once topped every list is barely remembered by 2025.

UK childhood obesity rates kept climbing through the 2010s, never reversing the trend officials warned about. Recent GLP-1 access programs for adults have begun to shift the national conversation, though child-specific treatment access remains limited.

In April 2018, the UK introduced a tax on sugary drinks, one of the first major measures of its kind. A wave of other countries have since rolled out similar taxes, though obesity rates have kept climbing almost everywhere regardless.

## 2. Pandemic, Public Health & Vaccines

Canada's national public health agency, built in the years after SARS exposed how little coordination existed between Ottawa and the provinces, became the backbone of the country's COVID-19 response in 2020, exactly the kind of national coordination Canada had lacked.

The mRNA technology Katalin Karikó couldn't get funded for years was used to build COVID-19 vaccines in under a year. She shared the 2023 Nobel Prize in Medicine for the discovery.

The funding-cycle warning came true almost exactly as predicted. Public health funding spiked during COVID and has since faded again, with research grants for future pandemic preparedness being cut in multiple countries by 2025.

Public trust in health agencies' vaccine guidance, once near record highs, has fallen sharply. The 2022 Ottawa trucker convoy made Canada's vaccine-mandate backlash a global news story, even though close to 90% of Canadian truckers were actually vaccinated at the time.

In November 2025, Canada officially lost its measles elimination status after sustained transmission for over a year, with more than 2,300 cases and two deaths recorded in the 2024 to 2025 outbreak. Measles, considered eliminated in Canada for decades, is no longer something Canadians can take for granted.

A WHO-coordinated international network for tracking disease outbreaks is still running a quarter-century after it launched, and it was activated repeatedly during COVID-19 and subsequent outbreaks, one of the quieter public health success stories of the past 25 years.

On March 11, 2020, the World Health Organization formally declared COVID-19 a global pandemic, the moment every public health system built up in the years before was put to its biggest test yet.

## 3. Mental Health

As part of a new national health funding deal in 2023, Ottawa earmarked dedicated money for mental health and substance use services as one of its named priorities, building on smaller bilateral funding agreements that began in 2017, decades after Canada first had no national mental health strategy at all.

Prescriptions for antidepressants have continued climbing, now joined by a much wider range of treatment options, including therapy delivered entirely through phone apps.

Public stigma around therapy and medication has dropped sharply over the past generation. Celebrities and athletes now routinely discuss their own mental health struggles in public, something almost unthinkable for a public figure not long ago.

Mental health coverage under most employer and provincial plans is still thinner than coverage for physical illness, even though demand for therapy has grown far faster than coverage limits have expanded.

Despite the cultural shift toward openness, rates of anxiety and depression among teenagers have risen sharply over the past two decades, not fallen, leading health officials in multiple countries to describe a youth mental health crisis by the early 2020s.

England's flagship anti-stigma mental health campaign shut down in 2021 after government funding was withdrawn, even as public attitude surveys showed the stigma-reduction work had succeeded faster than expected.

Workplace mental health is no longer an edge case. A growing number of countries, including France, Ireland, and parts of Canada, have passed formal right-to-disconnect laws, two decades after employers first started citing stress and burnout as a leading cause of leave.

## Noise (resolved)

Neither of these widely promoted ideas held up under real scrutiny.

Hydroxychloroquine was heavily promoted early in the COVID-19 pandemic as a possible treatment. Larger clinical trials later found no meaningful benefit, and the early hype quietly faded.

Ivermectin gained a large online following in 2021 as an unproven COVID-19 treatment. Major health agencies continued to find no solid evidence it worked, and the trend faded as more data came in.
