# Facilitator Reference: Future Thinking × Surgery
### Retrospective Foresight Activity — Early 2000s → Present Day

---

## How This Activity Works

Students receive 25 cards about surgery and healthcare from the early 2000s, spanning five formats: newspaper articles, forum threads, blog posts, tweets, and advertisements. The facts are real. Small details like names and bylines are constructed for the simulation.

Students Scan the cards, Cluster them into trends, then do history matching — make a prediction about what surgery looks like today and verify it through online research.

The cards are designed with noise mixed in, simulating how information actually looks in the real world. The noise is not misinformation. It is legitimate concerns raised at the time about cost, infrastructure, training gaps, patient acceptance, and competing platforms. Most of those voices were right about the problem. They were wrong about what it meant. The skill students are building is learning to tell the difference between a concern that describes a permanent limit and one that describes a temporary barrier.

## Activity Walkthrough

### 01 Scan — What signals stand out?

Going through all 25 cards, six things keep surfacing:

**Robots are entering the OR.** Da Vinci FDA cleared (N1), liver surgery completed robotically (N3), ads from Intuitive Surgical (A1). Not one mention but multiple independent sources across multiple years.

**Surgery is moving across distance.** The Lindbergh Operation (N2, T1) proves a surgeon can operate transatlantically. DARPA's Trauma Pod program is funding remote battlefield surgery, targeting a deployable system by 2010 (T10, B3 comments). Infrastructure conversations are starting (A4, T3).

**Training is broken and simulators might fix it.** Residents locked out of da Vinci (T8, F1, F3). Simulator ads appearing (A3). Gaming-surgery skill transfer data emerging (B1, F1, T9).

**The sense of touch is missing.** Blog B2 flags haptic feedback as the critical gap. Multiple forum replies echo the same thing: "you can't feel anything."

**The global access gap is being noticed.** T5 flags 1 vs 23 surgeons per 100,000. N4 connects telesurgery to equity. NorthernOntarioDoc in F2 puts a human face on it.

**Skepticism is loud but specific.** Critics are not saying the technology is wrong. They are saying: too expensive, learning curve too steep, infrastructure not ready, patients will not accept it. These are timing objections, not directional ones.

### 03 Imagine — Does history actually match?

**Trend A — Robotics expanding faster than training**

Confirmed and exceeded. Da Vinci is now in thousands of hospitals worldwide. Robotic surgery is standard for prostatectomy, hysterectomy, and colorectal procedures. The training gap was real, and simulation-based training became the answer. Surgical simulators are now mandatory in most residency programs, including at Temerty. The ZEUS platform died — da Vinci consolidated the market exactly as competitive dynamics suggested.

The noise cards (F3 — "burns out before 2010", N1 cost skeptics) were wrong on direction. Cost came down. Volume scaled. The marketing criticism was accurate but irrelevant to the trajectory.

**Trend B — Distance becoming irrelevant, infrastructure lagging**

Confirmed, with the lag the skeptics predicted. Telesurgery did not scale in the 2000s — Dr. Fenwick (N4) and OR_Realist (F2) were right that infrastructure and regulation were not ready. But in 2025, eight live telesurgeries were performed connecting France to hospitals across Asia. 5G is making dedicated fiber less necessary. The rural access argument (NorthernOntarioDoc, T5) is now the primary policy driver for telesurgery investment.

The noise cards (T2, T4, T7) were right about timing, wrong about direction. The technology worked. The infrastructure caught up.

**Trend C — The surgeon of the future looks different**

Largely confirmed. Simulation is now standard — the kind of simulator training the forum posts in F1 were speculating about is now embedded in residency programs worldwide, including at Temerty. The gaming-surgery link has been replicated across multiple studies. AR surgical overlays are in clinical trials. In 2022, a Johns Hopkins trial showed a robot autonomously suturing soft tissue with greater consistency than human surgeons in controlled conditions.

The noise here is worth noting. B3 (Hartley: "judgment can't be replaced") and B4 (Alderton: "patients won't accept it") were both partially right. Full autonomy has not arrived. But patient acceptance has been higher than Alderton predicted — surveys now show majority acceptance when outcomes are clearly explained.

## The ZEUS Moment

Card A2 is the most important noise card in the set.

ZEUS was a real robotic surgical system from Computer Motion Inc. with genuine clinical credibility. The Lindbergh Operation — the first transatlantic surgery referenced in N2 and T1 — was actually performed using a ZEUS system, not da Vinci. It was FDA-cleared, used at major hospitals, and looked like a strong signal in 2001. It was discontinued in 2003.

The ad itself makes this hard to miss: A2's own copy explicitly cites the Lindbergh Operation as proof of the platform ("Proven in the Lindbergh Operation: first transatlantic surgery") and quotes Dr. Marescaux directly. Students who cross-reference A2 against N2/T1 during Cluster will find a real, corroborated historical claim — which is exactly what makes this the card most likely to be read as durable signal instead of noise.

**Why da Vinci won:**

Da Vinci had more degrees of movement in its robotic arms and offered stereoscopic 3D vision from early on, while ZEUS used a 2D camera. Surgeons strongly preferred both. Intuitive Surgical also made a focused strategic decision: dominate one procedure first (radical prostatectomy), build deep loyalty with urologists, then expand. ZEUS spread across more procedures without owning any of them.

The decisive move was the 2003 acquisition. Both companies had been locked in years of patent litigation. Intuitive Surgical bought Computer Motion, ended the lawsuit, absorbed the patents and talent, and discontinued ZEUS simultaneously. Once hospitals invested in da Vinci training and service contracts, switching costs became enormous — creating a moat no new entrant could overcome.

**The lesson:** The best technology does not always win. The one that gets timing, focus, and market lock-in right does. Two signals can look identical — both real, both growing, both credible — and one is a dead end. The skill is asking what forces beyond the technology itself determine what survives: business model, patents, strategic focus, ecosystem lock-in.

ZEUS is the card most likely to fool students during Scan and Cluster. The debrief question worth sitting with: "If you were advising a hospital in 2002, which platform would you have bet on, and what would you have needed to know to get it right?"
